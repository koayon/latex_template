# Writing Abstracts

The abstract is one of the most important parts of the paper. Most people who come across your paper will only ever read the abstract (90%+). Many others will read the abstract and this will be a gating factor in whether they decide to read the rest of the paper or not. For this reason the abstract is one of the most high leverage points of the paper. For conferences/journals, the abstract is how they will assign your paper to reviewers with similar interests and the keywords in the abstract are what will be picked up when people search for your paper on Google/ChatGPT. These are the main audiences that you’re serving.

The abstract then has three main jobs: 
1. **Get the paper in front of your readers** - it should contain the exact match of keywords that you’re expecting your audience to be searching for
2. **Get the main message of the paper into your readers’ heads** - most people only read the abstract so we want to maximise the value that we give to these readers
3. **Intrigue readers into wanting to read the rest of the paper** - a well crafted abstract which explains the value of your contributions should make your target readers interested in understanding the details for which they’ll go to the main paper

To do these jobs successfully, given the importance of the abstract, the typical advice is that you should spend equal amounts of time on the Abstract/Title, Intro, Figures and Rest of Paper. I.e, 25% of your whole writing time spent on the abstract is likely a very good use of time as Abstract improvements are very highly leveraged. 

## Stages of Writing Your Abstract

### First Draft

I recommend writing the first version of the abstract very early: after you’ve written your pre_writing and outline but before you’ve written any of the paper and before you have all your experiments finished.
You should then write out the abstract that clearly details how you want to present the paper to the world. There are three main reasons for this. One is that its pretty motivating to know that you have the core story of the paper written down in a concise way that you’re excited about working on. The second is that you can show this abstract to collaborators or beta readers or anyone who wants some context on your project as a tight encapsulation of what you’re working on. The third and main reason is that having an abstract written down is highly focusing - if there are core experiments or writing that you’re about to do that don’t fit with the story of the abstract that fact should be highly obvious and jarring to you. You then have two options: 
1. Don’t engage in the work that would be out of scope for the structure of your paper
2. Or, engage in the work and then amend your abstract to note that you’ve changed the story
This means that you don’t unthinkingly do work that won’t be useful to your paper or will be confusing to your main narrative. 

### Writing the paper

Mostly when you’re writing the paper, you should leave the abstract alone and just have it as a guide like your outline.

### Polishing

When you’re done with your drafting then it’s a good time to return to your abstract and revise it in light of what you learned during writing the paper. This is where you’ll spend most of your abstract writing time. (Remember that Abstract Writing can productively take ~25% of your paper writing time.)

I would recommend doing this in 4 stages: 
1. Iterate on your current abstract based on the newfound wisdom you have after writing the paper. Make sure the abstract communicates the key points of the paper in the clearest way that you know how. 
2. Now have each of the co-authors of the paper write a new abstract of the paper without looking at the paper. Next have all the co-authors write a new abstract of the paper whilst actively referencing the paper and strictly following one of the Abstract guides here or here. It’s often useful to have both a freeform and a more structured version of the abstract to pull from both.
3. Put all these abstract versions in a Google Doc (along with abstracts that 3 LLMs wrote based on your paper) and comment on the parts that you like and dislike from each of them. Build up your updated abstract from taking the best parts from all of these
4. Finally ask some beta-readers to take a look at your abstract and give you detailed, critical feedback. Take their advice on board to further improve your abstract.

After this hopefully you have an abstract that you're happy with! Remember that iteration and continuous improvement is key to a great abstract.
