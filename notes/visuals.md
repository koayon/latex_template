# Creating Great Visuals and Captions

Visuals are a graphical interface between people and data. Think hard about the story that you want to tell with the visual. This will influence how you make all of your design decisions
- You can express the exact same data in many different ways depending on what you’re trying to emphasise
- If there’s one line/bar/dot that you want to draw people’s attention to ensure that this line is coloured/bolded appropriately so that people know that’s what they’re supposed to be drawn to
- You can use colour to make it clear what to contrast and what to view together
- Don’t use additional colour if there isn’t a comparison being made - everything that’s in a different colour will be interpreted as a contrast that you’re trying to draw
- Background details can be in grey as a neutral colour
- Always include a legend which describes the contrasts being drawn

Captions are an incredibly important part of your visual
- Ideally the visual and caption should be set up so that there’s a clear takeaway from looking at the visual for 5 seconds and that if you then read the caption you would confirm the same takeaway that you had which will help readers be confident they’re reading the visual correctly
- Captions should start with a declarative title sentence which says the one sentence takeaway. You can then use the rest of the caption to expand on this
  - E.g. “Figure 3: Sparse autoencoder features cluster by syntactic role” not “Figure 3: SAE features across widths from 1k-32k”.
  -  OR “Larger k improves validation loss but increases training instability” 
- Sometimes its useful to also include a sentence (typically the second sentence) which describes how to read the chart and what people are seeing in front of them (e.g. you can re-explain the legend here if its very complex but oftentimes you don’t need to)
- If there’s any detail in the visual that is abbreviated, it’s worth having it written out in full here so that people can understand the figure and caption outside of the context of the rest of the paper
- The rest of caption should all be exposition
- You should say what the method being used is in the caption and what the statistical results are (with numbers)
  - E.g. Our method outperforms Baseline A by 12.3% on Benchmark Z 

**Overall the aim is that just from seeing the main figure and chart (w/ captions), a reader could understand and reconstruct the main story**

The reason we use visuals is to express an idea in a way that is more easily digestible than with words alone
- Always think “how quickly are readers able to get to the idea?”
- (Of course this requires you to have a very clear idea in your mind of what the idea is that you’re trying to communicate - often this goes wrong because people add charts because “that’s what you do” rather than because they have some message to get across)
